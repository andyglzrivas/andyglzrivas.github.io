AI as a Progressive Force: Modernity and Labor Alienation

Durkheim's sociology of the division of labor offers a more demanding — and more useful — account of what AI is doing to work than the dominant narrative of disruption and displacement.

Andy Gonzalez Rivas
May 15, 2026 · 12 min read · SOC-355
Sociology Labor AI & Society Durkheim Modernity

Durkheim saw in the division of labor, developed to its fullest and governed by just institutions, not the destruction of human dignity but its condition. A society of generalists, each performing every function with equal mediocrity, was not a society of free persons — it was a society of interchangeable ones. Genuine individuality, genuine interdependence, genuine solidarity all require difference. It requires, in his terms, organic solidarity — the cohesion of a complex living system whose parts are distinguished precisely by what they uniquely contribute.

The rise of artificial intelligence as a productive force is generating, with remarkable consistency, a single dominant narrative: disruption, displacement, and deepening exploitation. Workers are surveilled by algorithmic management systems they cannot see or contest; cognitive labor is deskilled, standardized, and automated. These pathologies are real. But the narrative that treats them as the terminal diagnosis of AI's relationship to labor forecloses the analysis the moment demands. This essay argues that Durkheim's sociology of the division of labor provides precisely that analysis — that what we are witnessing is not the structural verdict of AI development but its anomic transition, and that the trajectory, properly understood, points toward the conditions of genuine organic solidarity rather than away from them.

The Division of Labor and Its Normal Form

Before we can understand the nature of the pathologies caused by the development of new technologies, we first need to understand the nature of the division of labor. Durkheim separates stages of societal development into modern and pre-modern. Mechanical solidarity is the cohesive force of pre-modern societies. It operates through resemblance — members of such a society share the same beliefs and practices. As the division of labor develops, difference emerges, and difference is precisely what mechanical solidarity cannot accommodate.

A new form of cohesion emerges to replace it: organic solidarity. In a mechanically solidary society, the individual has no real existence independent of the collective type. In an organically solidary society, each person occupies a unique functional position that no one else occupies. The more specialized the society, the more genuinely individuated its members, and the more robust the solidarity binding them — an argument that cuts directly against the humanist ideal of the fully developed person. For Durkheim, that ideal mistakes the individual for the organism: completeness belongs to the social body, not to any single cell within it. But this only holds when the organism is healthy.

Organic solidarity is the normal function of the division of labor — what it produces when it develops under adequate institutional conditions. For Durkheim, a social fact is normal when it serves a functional role at a given stage of societal development; its absence or distortion constitutes a pathology, not an indictment of the developmental tendency itself. The first and most socially pervasive abnormal form is anomie — the breakdown of normative regulation that occurs when the division of labor outpaces the institutional frameworks needed to integrate specialized functions into a coherent social whole.

Anomie is not the inevitable condition of differentiated societies; it is the specific pathology of societies whose moral and institutional development has not kept pace with the transformation of their productive forces. — Durkheim, The Division of Labor in Society, Book III

The second of the abnormal forms is that of forced division of labor. The division of labor generates organic solidarity only when people occupy functions their aptitudes genuinely fit — when specialization is experienced as self-expression rather than imposition. When workers are compelled into roles by structural inequality rather than aptitude, specialization becomes subjugation rather than self-expression, and the division of labor produces domination rather than solidarity.

AI as Anomic Transition

It is not the division of labor that has failed — it is the conditions under which it is currently developing. The intermediary institutions that would regulate AI-driven specialization and integrate it into a coherent social whole have not been built. What we are witnessing is anomie in a precise, technical sense: not chaos, but the specific pathology of a society whose moral and institutional development has not kept pace with the transformation of its productive forces. The labor phenomena we observe today — algorithmic management and robotic automation — are symptoms of the broader anomic pathology, not indictments of AI development generally.

The introduction of algorithmic management systems does not simply intensify managerial control; it transforms the worker–employer relationship into one that, in Durkheim's terms, lacks the minimal conditions of normative regulation. In warehouse and platform-based labor settings, performance metrics, task allocation, and disciplinary actions are increasingly mediated by opaque algorithms whose criteria are neither transparent nor negotiable. This opacity produces a structurally asymmetric field of knowledge: employers (or the systems acting on their behalf) possess total visibility into worker activity, while workers are denied access to the evaluative standards governing their own labor.

The result is not merely exploitation, but anomic disintegration. For Durkheim, a regulated relationship presupposes shared, intelligible norms that define obligations on both sides; where such norms are absent or illegible, coordination gives way to arbitrariness. Algorithmic management thus exemplifies anomie in a precise sense — not because it introduces disorder, but because it replaces socially mediated rules with inscrutable technical procedures. Crucially, however, this condition is not intrinsic to AI as a productive force. It reflects the absence of intermediary institutions capable of rendering these systems legible and contestable, suggesting that the pathology lies in institutional lag rather than technological development itself.

Robotic automation reorganizes the workplace around labor-saving technologies, displacing workers without regard for aptitude or expertise. Studies of robotized factories document deskilling and displacement from meaningful tasks. The options available to workers become limited to positions that require less skill and offer less agency — such as operating the equipment that displaced them — or sinking into unemployment and economic precarity. The further division of labor that emerges from these new technologies compels workers into degraded roles by structural absence of alternatives, not by aptitude.

The Institutional Remedy

What we have described is real. It is also, in Durkheim's terms, transitional — the expected pathology of a society whose productive forces have outrun its moral institutions, not the inevitable destination of AI development. The trajectory, properly understood, points elsewhere. The distinction between a transitional pathology and a structural one is not semantic — it is the difference between a society that must build new institutions and one that must abandon its organizing principle altogether. Durkheim's framework is unambiguous: anomie is remediable, and the remedy is institutional.

Durkheim identifies the professional corporation — an intermediary body between the isolated individual and the distant state — as the structural solution to anomie. Professional corporations set the norms governing specialized functions, regulate the terms of the worker-employer relationship, and provide the social integration that the division of labor by itself cannot generate. The corporation's central function is making the worker-employer relationship legible.

Introducing a workplace code of conduct for AI use performs exactly this function. It takes the opaque algorithmic management relationship and imposes normative structure on it — it allows workers to now see, understand, and contest the terms under which they are managed. Introducing AI ethics requirements into higher education would serve the function of a norm internalization mechanism. This means that AI regulation does not come from above — it is the cultivation of moral literacy that Durkheim identified as the precondition for genuine normative community. Academic AI disclosure agreements establish norms of transparency between those deploying AI systems and those subject to or affected by them.

The professional corporation does not only protect workers from employers — it creates obligations running in both directions, making the relationship genuinely normative rather than merely regulatory. These are all proposals that are currently being actively negotiated. The fact that the emerging AI regulation proposals operate at the same three levels as Durkheim's professional corporation is not coincidental — it is evidence that Durkheim's institutional logic is naturally asserting itself in practice.

Conclusion

What the preceding analysis demonstrates is that the contemporary experience of AI-driven labor is best understood not as a structural endpoint, but as a transitional pathology within the development of the division of labor. The anomic and forced forms examined here — opaque algorithmic management and the displacement of workers into degraded roles — do not represent the failure of differentiation itself, but the lag of moral and institutional regulation behind rapidly advancing productive forces. In Durkheim's terms, these conditions are diagnostic rather than definitive — they identify a breakdown in normative integration that is, in principle, remediable through the construction of intermediary institutions capable of rendering specialized relations legible and mutually binding.

Crucially, the institutional logic required for this transition is not hypothetical. The early formation of AI governance frameworks — ranging from workplace transparency standards to emerging professional and educational norms around responsible AI use — reflects an incipient movement toward precisely the kind of regulatory bodies Durkheim envisioned. These developments remain uneven and contested, but they indicate that the division of labor is not dissolving into permanent disintegration; rather, it is generating the conditions under which new forms of solidarity may be organized. AI as a progressive force, then, is neither a description of present reality nor a guarantee of future outcomes, but a Durkheimian conclusion grounded in the demonstrable tendency of complex societies to produce the institutions necessary for their own integration.
